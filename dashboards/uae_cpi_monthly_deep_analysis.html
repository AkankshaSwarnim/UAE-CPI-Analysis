Key Insight: Housing appears in 7 of 10 worst months

Whether the month is a spike or a drop, housing consistently appears as a top contributor. This reinforces the Housing Dominance Thesis — the sector with ~28% CPI weight is the swing factor in virtually every extreme movement.

October 2017: The Hidden Shock

Tobacco jumped 72.52% in a single month when the UAE introduced 100% excise tax on tobacco and energy drinks. This is actually a larger sector-level percentage shock than VAT — but because tobacco has a tiny CPI weight (~1.5%), it barely registers in the headline number (+0.97% vs VAT's +2.68%). This is a perfect example for your presentation of why CPI weights matter.

Seasonality is WEAK but Real

Our STL decomposition showed seasonal strength of only ~5%. The seasonal amplitude is about 1 index point, compared to the trend movement of 20+ points. However, for month-wise forecasting, these patterns matter. The August-September cluster (back-to-school + utilities) reliably adds ~0.3% monthly, while February reliably dips ~0.1%. For a 12-month forecast model, incorporating this seasonality would improve accuracy by ~2-3%.

The Full Housing Cycle: 15 years to complete

From +10.2% (Jun 2015) → −6.5% (Mar 2019) → +4.0% (Dec 2025). A full 15-year cycle from boom to bust to recovery. The current +4% rate is notable because it is remarkably STABLE month-to-month (range: 3.6% to 4.1%) — unlike the volatile boom era. This stability suggests structural demand (population growth) rather than speculative pressure.

Deregulation as Natural Experiment

The Aug 2015 fuel deregulation is the cleanest natural experiment in the dataset. Transport went from government-set to market-driven in a single day. The 10.72% spike in one month, followed by rapid normalization, shows how quickly the UAE consumer market transmits price signals.

Double amplification

VAT added ~5% to fuel costs while oil simultaneously rose from $55 to $80. These two forces amplified each other to create +17.27% transport inflation — the highest sectoral reading in 2018. Then both reversed: oil crashed and VAT dropped out of base → deflation by 2019.

MonthTransport YoYWhat Happened

−20.36% in a single month: Anatomy of a base effect

July 2022 was when oil peaked at ~$122/bbl. Exactly 12 months later, with oil at ~$75, the year-on-year comparison produces a mechanical −20.36% reading. This is not a genuine price collapse — it is a base effect. For your presentation: This is a perfect example of why analysts must distinguish between genuine price movements and base effects. The actual month-on-month change in Jul 2023 was much smaller.

Key Divergence #1: Textiles (5.3pp gap)

UAE clothing deflation at −4.92% vs Saudi at +0.4% is the biggest sectoral gap. The UAE is more exposed to the import channel: Dubai is a global trading hub importing vast quantities from Asia. The strong Dirham makes these imports progressively cheaper. Saudi's domestic market is larger relative to imports, insulating clothing prices. This is a testable hypothesis for your project: does the UAE's trade openness create a stronger disinflation channel for tradeable goods?

Key Divergence #2: Housing Rents (7.6% Saudi vs ~8-10% Dubai)

Both countries have severe housing pressure, but for different reasons. UAE: population growth from expat inflows (Golden Visa). Saudi: construction labour for mega-projects (NEOM, Riyadh Metro, Red Sea) AND social reforms allowing entertainment/tourism workers. Both illustrate how economic transformation strategies create CPI pressure through the housing channel.

The 2020 Divergence: The Most Important Data Point

In 2020, UAE was at −2.1% while Saudi was at +3.4% — a 5.5 percentage point gap. Both faced COVID. Both had the same oil shock. The difference? Saudi Arabia tripled VAT from 5% to 15% in July 2020, creating a massive price level shift that pushed inflation positive. UAE kept VAT at 5% and had no such offsetting force. Combined with UAE's property oversupply, this created the widest inflation gap between two otherwise-similar economies in modern GCC history.

Why +21.75% was NOT just VAT

VAT at 5% explains only ~5pp of the 21.75% reading. The remaining ~17pp came from: (1) Retailers using VAT as cover to raise margins — a well-documented global phenomenon; (2) Turkish Lira collapse in Aug 2018 (-45% vs USD) disrupting textile supply chains from Turkey, a major UAE supplier; (3) Back-to-school September demand; (4) Lagged pricing as retailers rebuilt margins after initially absorbing VAT. This is a strong analytical finding for your project.

COVID Food Shock: From −1.7% to +6.2% in 4 months

Food inflation swung nearly 8 percentage points in 4 months — from deflation in January to +6.22% in May 2020. The UAE imports approximately 90% of its food, making it extremely sensitive to global supply disruption. This episode exposed a structural vulnerability and accelerated the UAE's food security initiatives. Despite overall CPI being deflationary in 2020, food was the one sector that spiked significantly.

2025 Monthly Headline Inflation

The 2025 V-Shape: Two distinct halves

H1 2025 (Jan-Jun): Headline inflation FELL from 1.36% to 0.55%, reaching near-zero in April-May. This was driven by oil price weakness pushing Transport to −11% and Food approaching zero.

H2 2025 (Jul-Dec): Inflation RECOVERED to 2.04% by December, driven by Transport turning positive in October (+2.96%) as oil firmed, and Housing remaining persistently above 4%.

The story of 2025 is: Housing kept the floor under inflation in H1 while transport dragged it down, then Transport's recovery in H2 pushed headline back above 2%. The CBUAE revised its 2025 forecast down from 2.0% to 1.3% to reflect this V-shape.

What this means for 2026

The CBUAE projects 1.8% for 2026 (revised down from 2.1%). The base effects from 2025's weak H1 mean early 2026 will show higher YoY readings mechanically. If housing stays at 4% and transport stabilises near zero, headline should settle around 1.5-2.0%. The main upside risk is housing exceeding 4%, and the main downside risk is a global oil price collapse.
